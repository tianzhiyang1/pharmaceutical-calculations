Tutorial - IV Flow Rate and Admixture

I. Overview of Parenteral Administration

+
  • Parenteral dosage forms are administered outside the GI tract, typically by injection.
  • Routes include:
    • IV (Intravenous) – into the vein
    • IM (Intramuscular) – into the muscle
    • SC (Subcutaneous) – under the skin
    • Other specialized routes: intra-arterial, intraspinal, intra-articular, intracardiac

II. Types of IV Infusions

Type Description
Continuous Large-volume infusion over extended time (e.g., hydration, antibiotics)
Intermittent Smaller volume infused at intervals (e.g., IV piggyback for antibiotics)
IV Push Small volume injected over 1–5 minutes, often in emergencies

III. IV Flow Rate Basics

Definition:

The flow rate is how fast the IV fluid or drug is delivered into the bloodstream. It can be expressed in units such as mL/hour, mL/min, drops/min (gtt/min), mg/hour (for medication).

VI. IV Admixture Calculations

What is an IV admixture?

An IV admixture involves adding a specific volume of a drug (usually in solution form) to an IV fluid (e.g., D5W or NS) to prepare a medication for intravenous administration.

When are IV admixture calculations needed?

You will need to calculate how many milliliters (mL) of a drug solution should be added when:

  • A desired dose is prescribed (e.g., 1 gram lidocaine)
  • The drug is available in a known concentration (e.g., 50 mg per 10 mL)
  • You must determine the volume to add to the IV bag to supply that dose

Steps to calculate volume for IV admixtures

Step 1: Identify what are known
