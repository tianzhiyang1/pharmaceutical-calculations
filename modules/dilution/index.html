Tutorial - Dilution

Dilution: To diminish the strength of a preparation by the addition of solvent or vehicle.

Stock preparation: A relatively concentrated preparation of a medicinal or nonmedicinal substance used as the source of that substance to prepare a preparation of a lesser concentration.

Steps

1. Recognize that the problem involves dilution.

Look for key terms such as "diluted," "stock preparation," "final concentration," or "prepare from a stronger preparation." Dilution problems typically involve a higher concentration (stock) and a lower concentration (final product).

2. Understand the goal of the problem and determine what you are being asked to find. Identify the Q1, C1, Q2, and C2, and convert units as needed for the dilution formula
Q1 × C1 = Q2 × C2

  • Ensure quantities are all in the same unit (mL, L, g, etc.)
  • Ensure concentrations are in the same format (e.g., % to %, or ratio to ratio as fractions)

3. Use the dilution formula and solve for the unknown

Q1 × C1 = Q2 × C2

Example 1:

How many milliliters of 0.9% (w/v) sodium chloride ophthalmic solution can be prepared from 250 mL of 25% (w/v) solution? Round the final answer to the nearest whole number.

Step 1: Recognize that the problem involves dilution

Look for key terms such as "diluted," "stock preparation," "final concentration," or "prepare from a stronger preparation." Dilution problems typically involve a higher concentration (stock) and a lower concentration (final product).

Step 2: Understand the goal of the problem and determine what you are being asked to find. Identify the Q1, C1, Q2, and C2, and convert units as needed for the dilution formula Q1 × C1 = Q2 × C2

New concentration (C2) is given as 0.9% (w/v) sodium chloride ophthalmic solution; original quantity (Q1) is given as 250 mL; and original concentration (C1) is given as 25% (w/v) solution. Therefore:

Q1 = 250 mL, C1 = 25%

Q2 = ? (what we're solving for), C2 = 0.9%

All units are already in the correct format, so no conversion is needed.

Step 3: Use the dilution formula and solve for the unknown

Q1 × C1 = Q2 × C2

250 mL × 25% = Q2 × 0.9%

Q2 =
(250 × 25)
0.9
= 6,944.44 mL

Rounding to the nearest whole number: 6,944 mL

Example 2:

How many mL of a 1:400 (w/v) stock solution are needed to prepare 4 liters of a 1:2000 (w/v) solution?

Step 1: Recognize that the problem involves dilution:

Look for key terms such as "diluted," "stock preparation," "final concentration," or "prepare from a stronger preparation." Dilution problems typically involve a higher concentration (stock) and a lower concentration (final product).

Step 2: Understand the goal of the problem and determine what you are being asked to find. Identify the Q1, C1, Q2, and C2, and convert units as needed for the dilution formula Q1 × C1 = Q2 × C2

Q1 = ? (what we're solving for)

C1 = 1:400

Q2 = 4 liters = 4000 mL

C2 = 1:2000

Step 3: Use the dilution formula and solve for the unknown

Q1 × C1 = Q2 × C2

Q1 × 1/400 = 4000 mL × 1/2000

Q1 =
(4000 × 1/2000)
1/400
= 800 mL

Therefore, 800 mL of the 1:400 (w/v) stock solution is needed.
