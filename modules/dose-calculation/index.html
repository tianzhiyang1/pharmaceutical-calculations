Tutorial - Dose Calculations Using Body Weight, Creatinine Clearance, and BSA

I. Body weights and dosing calculations

Types of body weight measurements

Actual Body Weight (ABW) or Total Body Weight (TBW)

Actual weight measured on a scale.

Ideal Body Weight (IBW)

Estimated healthy body weight based on height and gender.

Adjusted Body Weight (AdjBW)

Used for dosing in obese patients when TBW significantly exceeds IBW.

1. Body Mass Index (BMI)

BMI = Weight (kg) [Height (m)]²

OR BMI = Weight (lbs) × 703 [Height (inches)]²

2. Ideal body weight (IBW)

Males: IBW = 50 + 2.3 x (inches over 5 ft)

Females: IBW = 45.5 + 2.3 x (inches over 5 ft)

3. Adjusted body weight (AdjBW, factor = 0.4)

AdjBW0.4 = IBW + 0.4 x (TBW - IBW)

4. Choosing the Right Weight for Drug Dosing

  1. Compare TBW and IBW.
  2. If TBW is close to IBW (normal or underweight), use TBW for most medications.
  3. If the patient is obese (TBW > 120% of IBW), use:
    • IBW for drugs such as aminophylline, acyclovir, and levothyroxine.
    • AdjBW for aminoglycosides (e.g., tobramycin).
    • TBW for drugs like vancomycin and heparin.
  4. Follow institutional policy, exam instructions, and current clinical guidelines.

Example 1 (use TBW)

A 45-year-old female is admitted with sepsis. She is 5′6″ tall and weighs 175 lb. She is to receive a loading dose of vancomycin at 25 mg/kg.

Hint: Vancomycin dosing is based on total body weight (TBW), even in obese patients.

What dose should she receive? Round to the nearest 250 mg, and do not exceed 2000 mg.

Step 1: Calculate TBW and IBW

TBW = 175 lb ÷ 2.2 = 79.5 kg

IBW = 45.5 + (2.3 × 6) = 59.3 kg

Step 2: Determine if patient is obese

TBW/IBW = 79.5 / 59.3 = 1.34 → she is 134% of her IBW → Obese, but vancomycin uses TBW

Step 3: Calculate dose

Dose = 25 mg/kg × 79.5 kg = 1987.5 mg → round to 2000 mg

Final Answer: 2000 mg

Example 2 (use IBW)

A female patient is prescribed theophylline 5 mg/kg/day. She is 5′7″ and weighs 243 lb.

Hint: For theophylline, use IBW in obese patients.

What is her daily dose? Round to the nearest whole mg.

Step 1: Calculate TBW and IBW

TBW = 243 ÷ 2.2 = 110.5 kg

IBW = 45.5 + (2.3 × 7) = 61.6 kg

Step 2: Determine if patient is obese

TBW/IBW = 110.5 / 61.6 = 1.79 → Obese, so use IBW

Step 3: Calculate dose

Dose = 5 mg/kg × 61.6 kg = 308 mg/day

Final Answer: 308 mg/day

Example 3 (use AdjBW)

A 34-year-old male (6′7″, 287 lb) is prescribed tobramycin 2 mg/kg IV Q8H for a serious infection.

Hint: For aminoglycosides like tobramycin, use AdjBW if the patient is obese.

What dose should he receive? Round to the nearest 10 mg.

Step 1: Calculate TBW and IBW

TBW = 287 ÷ 2.2 = 130.5 kg

IBW = 50 + (2.3 × 19) = 93.7 kg

Step 2: Determine if patient is obese

TBW/IBW = 130.5 / 93.7 = 1.39 → he is 139% of his IBW or 39% above his IBW → obese, so use AdjBW

Step 3: Calculate AdjBW

AdjBW = 93.7 + 0.4 × (130.5 − 93.7) = 108.4 kg

Step 4: Calculate dose

Dose = 2 mg/kg × 108.4 kg = 216.8 mg → round to 220 mg IV Q8H

Final Answer: 220 mg IV Q8H

II. Creatinine Clearance (CrCl) and Renal Drug Dosing

Cockcroft & Gault Equation:

Male: CrClmale = (140 – Age) × Weight in kg 72 × SCr

Female: CrClfemale = 0.85 × CrClmale = 0.85 × (140 – Age) × Weight in kg 72 × SCr

Which weight to use:

  • Underweight: Use actual body weight (ABW)
  • Normal weight: Use ideal body weight (IBW)
  • Overweight (BMI ≥ 25): Use adjusted body weight (AdjBW)

Additional notes on CrCl estimation (institutional variability):

Depending on institutional policy and clinical context, the following considerations may apply:

  • Use of AdjBW in the Cockcroft-Gault Equation:
    Many institutions recommend using AdjBW when ABW exceeds IBW by ≥30%, rather than relying solely on a BMI ≥25.
  • Low serum creatinine in elderly or cachectic patients:
    In older adults (≥65 years) or cachectic individuals (ABW ≥20% below IBW), a serum creatinine (SCr) < 1 mg/dL may underestimate renal function. Some clinicians round SCr up to 1 mg/dL to avoid overestimation of creatinine clearance.
  • Short stature adjustments:
    For patients under 5 feet tall, standard IBW formulas may overestimate ideal weight.
    • IBW is calculated by subtracting from 50 (male) or 45.5 (female), down to 4’10’’.
    • For individuals under 4’10’’: some protocols recommend capping IBW at 45.5 kg for males and 40.9 kg for females.
    • Alternatively, for height below 4’10’’, use Jelliffe equation; Schwartz equation may be considered for more accurate estimation in the pediatric population.

Evaluating Kidney Function: Shift from Cockcroft-Gault to eGFR

  • eGFR (Estimated Glomerular Filtration Rate)
    • Newer method, based on diverse populations
    • Uses age, sex, serum creatinine (no race in latest version)
    • Based on standardized serum creatinine
    • Auto-reported by labs and preferred for CKD staging/dosing
  • Cockcroft-Gault (CrCl)
    • Older method (1976), requires manual calculation
    • Uses age, weight, sex, serum creatinine
    • Not based on standardized serum creatinine
    • Still referenced in some older FDA drug labels
  • Why eGFR Is Preferred Today
    • More accurate and consistent with lab tests
    • Does not require weight
    • Already used for CKD staging
    • Supported by updated KDIGO 2024 guidelines
    • 2021 CKD-EPI equation is current gold standard (as of 2025)
Note: Some drug labels still use CrCl, so pharmacists must be familiar with both methods.

Example:

A 64-year-old female (5′5″, 205 lb) is hospitalized with nosocomial pneumonia and is receiving ciprofloxacin, Primaxin, and vancomycin. Morning labs: K 4 mEq/L, BUN 60 mg/dL, SCr 2.7 mg/dL, glucose 222 mg/dL.

Based on the renal dosing table below, what is the correct Primaxin dose for this patient?

CrCl ≥ 71 mL/min 41 - 70 mL/min 21 - 40 mL/min 6 - 20 mL/min
Primaxin Dose 500 mg IV Q6H 500 mg IV Q8H 250 mg IV Q6H 250 mg IV Q12H
Step 1: Figure out which weight to use for CrCl

BMI = 205/65² × 703 = 34.1 kg/m²; obese → so use AdjBW for CrCl

Step 2: Calculate AdjBW

TBW = 205 ÷ 2.2 = 93.18 kg

IBW = 45.5 kg + (2.3 × 5 in) = 57 kg

AdjBW0.4 = 57 + 0.4 × (93.18 - 57) = 71.47 kg

Step 3: Use Cockcroft & Gault equation to calculate CrCl

CrCl = 0.85 × (140 – 64) × 71.47 72 × 2.7 = 23.75 mL/min

Step 4: Check on the package labeling info, and determine the dose

CrCl = 23.75 mL/min falls in the 21-40 mL/min range

The dose is 250 mg IV Q6H

III. Body Surface Area (BSA) and Drug Dosing

Mosteller Equation:

BSA (m²) = Weight (kg) × Height (cm) 3600

OR

BSA (m²) = Weight (lbs) × Height (in) 3131

BSA is primarily used for chemotherapy and pediatric dosing.

Example:

A breast cancer regimen includes doxorubicin 30 mg/m²/day for 3 days. How much should a patient who is 5′6″ (167.6 cm) and 144 lb (65.5 kg) receive per day?

Step 1: Use the Mosteller equation to calculate BSA; and convert height/weight if needed

Wt = 144 lb × 1 kg/2.2 lb = 65.5 kg

Ht = 66 inches × 2.54 cm/1 inch = 167.6 cm

BSA = √(65.5 × 167.6)/3600 = 1.74 m²

Step 2: Calculate dose

Dose = 30 mg/m²/day × 1.74 m² = 52.2 mg/day

Final Answer: 52.2 mg/day
