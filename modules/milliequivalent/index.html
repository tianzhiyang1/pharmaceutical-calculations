Tutorial - Milliequivalent

I. Concept review:

+

Non-electrolytes: These compounds dissolve in solution as whole molecules. The osmotic pressure of their solutions depends solely on the solute concentration.

Electrolytes: Electrolytes dissociate into ions in solution. Their osmotic pressure depends on both the solute concentration and the degree of dissociation. Because ionized particles exert a greater number of osmotic particles, electrolytes generally contribute more to osmotic pressure than non-electrolytes.

Importance in the Body:

Electrolytes help maintain:

  • Acid–base balance
  • Water distribution and volume
  • Neuromuscular function
  • Metabolic processes

Electrolyte concentrations are commonly expressed in equivalents (Eq) or milliequivalents (mEq) per liter.

Milliequivalent (mEq):

  • Represents 1/1000 of a gram equivalent weight of a substance.
  • Reflects the chemical activity of electrolytes, taking into account both mass and valence.
  • mEq of a compound = mEq of the cation = mEq of the anion, although the mass of ions will differ.

Where:

  • Molecular Weight (MW) refers to the formula weight of the compound, expressed in milligrams (mg).
  • Valence is the number of positive or negative charges carried by the ion (e.g., Na⁺ = 1, Ca²⁺ = 2, SO₄²⁻ = 2).

You may also encounter the following alternative, but equivalent, formula formats:

mEq =
mg × valence
MW
OR mEq = mmols × valence

Where:

  • mg = mass of the substance in milligrams
  • MW = molecular weight in mg/mmol
  • mmol = millimoles (mg ÷ MW)

These equations allow you to interconvert between mass, millimoles, and milliequivalents based on the ion's valence.

III. How to determine valence

Valence = number of electrical charges on either cation or anion.

Example 1: NaCl

Na⁺ = +1, Cl⁻ = -1 → Valence = 1

Example 2: CaCl₂

Ca²⁺ = +2, 2Cl⁻ = -2 → Valence = 2
