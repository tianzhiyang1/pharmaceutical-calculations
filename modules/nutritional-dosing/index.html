Tutorial - Nutritional Dosing

I. Nutritional Requirements

  • Macronutrients: Proteins, lipids (fats), and carbohydrates
  • Micronutrients: Vitamins, minerals, and electrolytes
  • These nutrients are essential for maintaining or achieving the desired nutritional status.
  • Requirements vary based on age, gender, physical parameters, disease state, and current nutritional status

II. Routes of Nutrient Delivery

Enteral Nutrition

  • Nutrients delivered through the gastrointestinal (GI) tract
  • Includes oral intake or tube feeding with liquid formulations
  • Preferred when the GI tract is functional
  • Advantages: Lower cost, fewer complications

Parenteral Nutrition

  • Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN): Nutrients delivered intravenously
  • Indicated when GI absorption is not possible
  • General rule: "If the gut works, use it"

III. Caloric Requirements

  • Energy needs vary with physical condition and illness severity
  • Kilocalorie (kcal): Heat required to raise the temperature of 1 kg of water by 1°C
  • Basal Energy Expenditure (BEE) is the energy required at rest to maintain basic physiological functions
    • Also called Resting Metabolic Energy (RME) or Resting Energy Expenditure (REE)
    • Estimated at 15–25 kcal/kg/day

IV. Harris-Benedict Equation (to Estimate BEE)

Male: BEE = 66 + (13.7 × W) + (5 × H) – (6.8 × A)

Female: BEE = 655 + (9.6 × W) + (1.8 × H) – (4.7 × A)

Where:

  • W = weight (kg)
  • H = height (cm)
  • A = age (years)

V. Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDE)

Total daily energy expenditure (TDE) = BEE × activity factors × stress factors

Activity Factors:

  • Confined to bed: 1.2
  • Ambulatory: 1.3

Stress Factors:

  • Non-stressed: 1.0
  • Surgery: 1.2
  • Infection: 1.4–1.6
  • Trauma: 1.3–1.5
  • Burns: 1.5–2.1

Steps for BEE and TDE Calculations

Step 1: Gather patient information and convert units if necessary

  • Age (in years)
  • Weight (in kg)
  • Height (in cm)
  • Sex (male or female)

Step 2: Calculate BEE using the Harris-Benedict equation

  • Male: BEE = 66+(13.7 × W)+(5 × H)–(6.8 × A)
  • Female: BEE = 655+(9.6 × W)+(1.8 × H)–(4.7 × A)

Note: BEE is expressed in kcal/day

Step 3: Apply activity and stress factors to find TDE (patient's total energy needs per day).

Total daily energy expenditure (TDE) = BEE x activity factors x stress factors

Example 1

Using the Harris-Benedict equation and assuming no stress factor, calculate the BEE (basal energy expenditure) for a 58-year-old female who is 5'3" tall and weighs 140 lb.

Step 1: Gather patient information and convert units if necessary
  • A = 58 yr
  • H = 5'3" = 63 in x 2.54 cm/1 in = 160 cm
  • W = 140 lb x 1 kg/2.2 lb = 63 kg
  • Female
Step 2: Calculate BEE using the Harris-Benedict equation

Female: BEE = 655+(9.6 × W)+(1.8 × H)–(4.7 × A)

BEE = 655 + (9.6 × 63) + (1.8 × 160) – (4.7 × 58)

= 1275.2 kcal/day

Example 2

A 25 year old female major trauma patient survives surgery and is recovering in the surgical intensive care unit. The medical team wants to start TPN. She is 122 pounds, 5'7'' with some mild renal impairment. Calculate her BEE using the Harris Benedict equations and her TDE non-protein caloric requirements (stress factor = 1.7 and activity factor = 1.2)

Step 1: Gather patient information and convert units if necessary

  • A = 25 yr
  • H = 5'7" = 67 in x 2.54 cm/1 in = 170 cm
  • W = 122 lb x 1 kg/2.2 lb = 55.5 kg
  • Female

Step 2: Calculate BEE using the Harris-Benedict equation

Female: BEE = 655+(9.6 × W)+(1.8 × H)–(4.7 × A)

BEE = 655 + (9.6 × 55.5) + (1.8 × 170) – (4.68 × 25)

= 1377 kcal/day

Step 3: Apply activity and stress factors to find TDE

Total daily energy expenditure (TDE) = BEE × activity factors × stress factors

TDE = BEE × activity factor × stress factor

= 1377 × 1.2 × 1.7

= 2809 kcal/day

Summary of Roles:

  • Carbohydrates – Primary energy source
  • Fats – Secondary energy source; vital for hormones, cell membranes, and fat-soluble vitamins
  • Proteins – Used for muscle building/repair or energy if carbs/fats are insufficient

Steps for Caloric Conversion Questions

These questions typically ask two types of questions:

  • how many kcal are provided by a given volume or weight of a macronutrient
  • how much of a nutrient is needed to meet a target kcal amount

Step 1: Know the caloric conversion values

Macronutrient Enteral kcal/g Parenteral kcal/g or mL
Carbohydrate 4 kcal/g 3.4 kcal/g
Protein 4 kcal/g 4 kcal/g
Fat (lipid) 9 kcal/g 10%: 1.1 kcal/mL
20%: 2 kcal/mL
30%: 3 kcal/mL

Step 2: Use ratio and proportion (or dimensional analysis) for caloric conversions

  • To calculate kcal from g or mL:
    Set up the proportion: kcal per g or mL / 1 g or mL = x kcal / given grams or mL
    Then solve for x (the number of kcal)
  • To calculate grams or mL needed to provide specific kcal:
    kcal per g or mL / 1 g or mL = target kcal / x g or mL
    Then solve for x (the amount of nutrient needed)

Step 3 (if needed): Apply to each macronutrient separately

When multiple components are present (e.g., dextrose, amino acids, and lipids in TPN), calculate kcal for each, then add kcal from each macronutrient to determine the total energy provided.

Total kcal = kcalcarbs + kcalprotein + kcalfat
