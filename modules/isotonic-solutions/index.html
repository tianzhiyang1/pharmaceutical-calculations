Tutorial - Isotonic Solution

I. Concept Review

+

Osmosis: Osmosis is the movement of a solvent (usually water) across a semipermeable membrane from a dilute solution to a more concentrated one, aiming to equalize concentrations on both sides of the membrane.

Osmotic pressure: Osmotic pressure is the force required to prevent osmosis. It is directly proportional to the number of solute particles in solution - not the type of particles.

Osmotic vs. Tonic

When referring to biological membranes (e.g., cell membranes), the term tonicity is used instead of osmotic pressure.

  • Tonicity describes the effect of a solution on water movement across a biological membrane.
  • It depends on the relative concentration and number of solute particles on either side of the membrane.

Isosmotic vs. Isotonic

  • Isosmotic: Two solutions with the same osmotic pressure, regardless of biological compatibility.
  • Isotonic: A solution with the same osmotic pressure as body fluids, with physiological compatibility.

Classifications:

  • Isotonic: No net water movement; cells remain normal.
  • Hypertonic: Higher osmotic pressure than body fluids; water exits the cells → cells shrink.
  • Hypotonic: Lower osmotic pressure than body fluids; water enters the cells → cells swell and may rupture.

Effect on Red Blood Cells (RBCs):

  • Isotonic solution: No net water movement → RBC remains unchanged.
  • Hypotonic solution: Water influx → cell swells → possible lysis.
  • Hypertonic solution: Water efflux → cell shrinks.

II. Importance of Isotonic Solutions in Pharmacy

Isotonic solutions are essential in pharmaceutical formulations to avoid irritation or damage to tissues. These include:

  • Ophthalmic preparations
  • Nasal sprays
  • Injectable (parenteral) formulations
  • Rectal preparations (e.g., enemas)

To achieve isotonicity, we use adjustment methods. The Sodium Chloride Equivalent Method is the most commonly used, though Freezing Point Depression is also employed.

III. Sodium Chloride Equivalent (E-Value) Method

This method adjusts the tonicity of a solution to be equivalent to 0.9% NaCl, which is isotonic with body fluids. The adjustment can be made using NaCl or other agents such as boric acid or dextrose.

  • Normal Saline (0.9% NaCl w/v) = isotonic with body fluids
  • Many simple drug solutions are hypotonic and require the addition of tonicity-adjusting agents.

Sodium Chloride Equivalent (E-value)

The E-value is the amount of sodium chloride (in grams) that is osmotically equivalent to 1 gram of a given drug.

Example E-values:

  • Atropine sulfate: E = 0.12
  • Boric acid: E = 0.52
  • Dextrose (anhydrous): E = 0.18

IV. Calculating the E-value (when not provided)

You can calculate the E-value using:

E =
58.5 × idrug
MWdrug × 1.8

Where:

  • 58.5 = Molecular weight of NaCl
  • 1.8 = dissociation factor of NaCl
  • MW = Molecular weight of the drug
  • i = dissociation factor of the drug

Example:

A new drug, Utopical, MW 175 and dissociation factor i = 3.4, is to be provided as 325 mg in 60 mL of solution made isotonic with sodium chloride. Calculate the E value of Utopical.

E =
58.5 × 3.4
175 × 1.8
= 0.63
